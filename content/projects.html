Inequality in the NBA draft

Inspired by research investigating institutional inequality in academic publishing and hiring, I wrote a report on the distribution of U.S. colleges in the NBA draft. I discovered that the proportion of picks from the top 10 schools has steadily increased since the one-and-done rule came into effect in 2006. I also investigated if attending a blue blood translates to higher performance at the professional level. The short answer is that it may matter somewhat, but not for the most talented players.

Home Advantage and NBA Free Throw Accuracy

I had the pleasure of taking Susan Athey and Stefan Wager’s course on Machine Learning and Causal Inference this past Spring. For the final project, I co-wrote an analysis of the causal effect of home advantage on NBA free throw accuracy. We estimated average and heterogeneous treatment effects using AIPW, Honest Causal Trees, Causal Forests, and meta-learners. Once we controlled for confounders such as travel effects, we found little significant evidence that the away team’s lower free throw percentages are caused by playing in front of opposing fans. Two interesting cases of heterogeneity: the visiting team’s free throw accuracy drops (1) when they are getting blown out and (2) when the game is nearly over.

True Prevalence of Nationalistic Language in 21C Fiction

Do “minor” literatures have to be more nationalistic to be successful? Andrew Piper and I worked on estimating the true prevalence of national place mentions using Bayesian methods in Stan based on this paper. This approach can account for the sensitivity and sensitivity of an NER platform and can be employed for both binomial and hypergeometric sampling procedures.

Topic Probabilities in Academic Journals

Professor Piper and I investigated the rise and fall of topics (e.g. cultural studies, gender studies, race) in humanities journals since the 1950s. Some of these topics’ rates of appearance in academic articles can be well-modeled by sigmoid and multi-linear curves since the life-cycle for relevance often follows one of two simple life-cycles: (1) obscurity, rise, and saturation; (2) prominence, fall, and dissipation. Here’s what the topic probabilities for words related to “cultural studies” looks like. Here’s a blog post by Professor Piper on some related work on gender studies.

Bob Dylan: Nobel Prize-Winning Poet

Bob Dylan was a surprise (and controversial) pick for the 2016 Nobel Prize in literature. After scraping all his lyrics, I compared their word frequency distributions to those of other prize-winning poets, including fellow laureates Yeats, Eliot, and Heaney, as well as Dylan’s pal Allen Ginsberg. Turns out “Visions of Johanna” and “Desolation Row” hold their own among 20C verse, however most of Dylan’s diction differs from traditional poetic lexicon. (I’ll blame his 80’s output, especially this.) The analysis can be found here.
